# Note 1: Data QC and Integration Decisions

## Purpose

This note records the first major decision layer of the project: how the six samples were read, filtered, normalized and integrated before downstream annotation.

The goal of this stage was not to produce the cleanest possible object by aggressive filtering, but to obtain a reliable working atlas while preserving potential APAP-related biological differences.

## Dataset Overview

The project uses GSE228305, including 3 Control and 3 APAP mouse liver non-parenchymal cell samples.

After QC, the integrated object contained 88,060 cells and 24,873 genes. Cell numbers were uneven across samples: `con1` and `AP-300` had around five thousand cells, while the other samples had around sixteen to twenty-two thousand cells. This imbalance is important because sample-level analyses should not be replaced by pooled-cell comparisons.

## QC Strategy

QC was based on three standard metrics:

- `nFeature_RNA`
- `nCount_RNA`
- `percent.mt`

The filtering strategy was intentionally conservative. In exploratory single-cell analysis, overly strict early filtering may remove stressed or injury-related cells, especially in a disease model such as APAP-induced liver injury.

The main principle was:

> Filter obvious low-quality cells, but do not remove borderline populations before seeing their marker structure and sample distribution.

This is why QC plots before and after filtering were kept as formal outputs rather than treated as temporary checks.

## Integration Strategy

Harmony was applied using `sample_id` as the integration variable. The group label, Control vs APAP, was not used as the correction variable because APAP-related variation is the biological signal of interest.

The intended balance was:

- remove sample-specific technical effects;
- retain disease-related biological differences;
- avoid forcing Control and APAP into complete overlap.

After Harmony, major UMAP structures were shared across samples, suggesting that severe sample-specific batch separation was reduced. At the same time, group-associated enrichment in some regions remained visible, supporting the decision to continue with downstream annotation.

## PCA and UMAP Decisions

Thirty PCs were used as a moderate exploratory choice. The elbow plot showed a fast drop in the early PCs followed by a longer tail. Using too few PCs may lose rare cell populations, while using too many may introduce noise into clustering and UMAP.

UMAP plots were generated by sample and by group. For dense single-cell plots, group-level plots can be visually misleading because later-drawn cells cover earlier ones. Faceted or shuffled plots are therefore more reliable for assessing whether both groups cover the same global structure.

## Risk Points Kept for Later Stages

Several issues were carried forward into later interpretation:

- `con1` and `AP-300` had much lower cell numbers than the other samples.
- Cell proportion changes must be interpreted at sample level, not pooled-cell level.
- Harmony may reduce technical differences, but it cannot prove that all biological and technical variation has been separated.
- QC filtering does not fully solve ambient RNA, doublets or stressed-cell artifacts.

These risks directly influenced later choices: the project emphasized sample-level composition, pseudobulk differential expression and cautious interpretation rather than strong claims from pooled single-cell statistics.

## Technical Notes

Several implementation issues were encountered and fixed:

- `RunHarmony()` argument names differed across package versions, so the script was adjusted to match the installed Harmony interface.
- Large Seurat objects caused memory pressure on macOS, so the workflow used sequential execution and scaled only variable features.
- QC summary tables required explicit numeric conversion from R `table` objects to avoid malformed output.

These are not central biological findings, but they are useful records because they reflect real reproducibility problems in single-cell projects.
